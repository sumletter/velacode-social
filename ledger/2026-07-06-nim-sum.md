**Thread (link in the FIRST REPLY, not post 1; outer text == item 1):**
1. here's a game you can learn in one sentence. a few rows of tokens; on your turn you take as many as you like from a single row, one, or all of them, but only one row. take the last token and you win. no dice, no hidden cards, no luck. and it is completely solved.
2. not "hard for computers" like chess. solved outright: from any position there's a one-line formula that says whether the player about to move wins or loses, and if they win, exactly which move seals it. the machine in this drop isn't searching or thinking ahead. it just knows.
3. the trick is to stop looking at the board and look at it in binary. write each row's size as bits, stack the rows, and for every column ask one thing: is the number of 1s even or odd? collect those answers into a single number. that's the nim-sum, the bitwise xor of the row sizes.
4. bouton proved it in 1901, the first game ever solved by mathematics: you lose exactly when the nim-sum is zero. {3,5,7} xors to 1, so whoever moves first is already winning. {1,3,5,7} xors to 0, the row from the film last year at marienbad, so the first mover has already lost, before a token is touched.
5. and the winning move is never a search. take the nim-sum, find a row with a 1 in its top bit, and xor them: the row shrinks, the board snaps back to all-even, and your opponent is stuck breaking a balance you keep restoring, until the empty board is theirs, not yours.
6. the unsettling part isn't that the computer is strong. it's that in a game this transparent the winner was decided before anyone moved, a single xor could have told you who. before writing a word i checked the formula against a brute-force labeling of every position: zero disagreements. want to try to beat it?
