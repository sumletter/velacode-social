## Threads, hero card + thread (slides 01–04, JPEG, PER-ITEM on thread[0].assets)
**Card:** slide-01.jpg (hero) leads; slides 02–04 ride the same first item.

**Thread (link in the FIRST REPLY, not post 1; outer text == item 1):**
1. here's a test that's 99% accurate. it looks for a disease you have a 1-in-1,000 chance of carrying. you take it, it comes back positive, and your real chance of actually being sick is about 9%. one in eleven. it's not a typo, and nothing about the test is broken.
2. don't trust the percentage, count people. take 10,000. about 10 really have it, and the test catches all 10. but it also wrongly flags 1% of the 9,990 healthy people: about 100 false alarms. so 110 people hear the alarm and only 10 are truly sick. 10 in 110 is roughly 9%.
3. the thing running that answer isn't the test's accuracy. it's how rare the disease is, the base rate. when there are barely any sick people to find and a huge crowd of healthy ones, even a tiny error rate buries the real cases under false ones. ignore it and you land confidently, precisely wrong.
4. so is a positive useless? no, it just isn't a verdict. hold the test fixed and a positive means almost nothing on a rare disease and almost everything on a common one. same test, opposite meaning, depending only on who took it. it's why a doctor tests a hunch, not the whole street.
5. and the real fix is to test again. each independent positive multiplies your odds by the same amount, so weak evidence stacked becomes strong, a rare-disease prior can climb from a few percent past 50% and on toward certainty. bayes isn't one answer you get once; it's a running total.
6. you can drag the three dials yourself in the drop, prevalence, sensitivity, specificity, and watch 10,000 dots recolor and the odds recompute live. nothing fetched, nothing stored. what prevalence would it take before you'd trust a single positive?
