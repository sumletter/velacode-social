## Threads, hero card + thread (slides 01–04, JPEG, PER-ITEM on thread[0].assets)

**Thread (link in the FIRST REPLY, not post 1; outer text == item 1):**
1. here is a whole game. two piles of stones. on your turn you take any number from one pile, or the same number from both piles at once, and whoever takes the last stone wins. that is the entire rulebook. drop the both-piles move and it is just nim, a game i have shipped three times. leave it in and something strange happens.
2. in plain two-pile nim the losing positions are the ones that xor to zero: write both piles in binary and every column comes out even. that one trick has run through every stone game on this site so far. add the move that lets you take from both piles at once and the xor rule quietly stops working. what takes its place is not more arithmetic.
3. it is the golden ratio. list the losing squares, the ones you never want handed to you, and plot them: they fall on two straight lines climbing out of the corner, slopes φ and 1/φ, forever creeping toward the diagonal without touching it. the k-th one is floor(k·φ) and floor(k·φ²). a token game, ruled by an irrational number.
4. the reason is a small piece of magic called beatty's theorem: pick two irrationals whose reciprocals add to 1, and the floors of their multiples split the whole number line with no gap and no overlap. only one pair closes that loop on itself, φ and φ², because 1/φ + 1/φ² = 1. that identity is the entire solution, folded into one line.
5. the nice part is i never had to trust any of it. i built the machine's cold list by a plain greedy rule, one integer at a time, no decimals, then checked it: it matches true perfect play on every board up to (40,40), 0 mismatches over 861 pairs, and the golden-ratio formula reproduces it exactly. play 'corner the queen' and you will lose. the machine will even show you the one square that would have saved you.
6. → velacode.xyz/drops/wythoff
